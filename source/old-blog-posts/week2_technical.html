Padding and Border and Margin. Oh my.

Alex Birdsall
5.25.2014

Welcome to the Box Model. It's the basic, generic structure of HTML elements. Each part of a website's HTML structure is built like this, so it's worth getting your head around a few of its quirks. First, though, take a look:

Let's start with the light blue box in the center. That's your content. What exactly it is depends on the type of element: the text of the paragraph in a <p> tag, the image in an <img> tag, and so on. This is the starting point. If you use CSS to explicitly set the width*, this is the box you're sizing. Take note: if you set that width declaration to the entire space available, any padding, margin, or border on top of that will make your element overfill its space. This can cause havoc to a carefully-designed layout. So, having established the content box as a starting point, what's all that stuff around it all about?

The next piece to consider is the border. This one is fairly self-explanitory: it's a line around the outside of the element, which can serve as both demarcation and decoration. Background styling like background-color surrounds the content and ends here, at the border. For purposes of illustration, here's a <div> with a width of 300px, a background-color of aqua, and with border, padding, and margin all set to none:

Nicccccce. Notice that even though the width hasn't changed, the element as a whole is, well, wider. Careful of that. Anyway, now we're ready for the last, outermost box in our model: the margin. Margin works almost exactly like padding, with a few key differences:

  • It's outside of the border, not in, which means:
  • Element-specific styling like our good friend background-color don't affect the margin. It's invisible! It looks just like whatever's holding the element.
  • Vertical margins overlap. More on this later.
So. Just to make it obvious, let's give our <div> a margin of 50px:

And lo, our <div> is set 50px away from the left side of this column, where before, it was 0px away, lined up with the far left edge of the text. It's 50px above this paragraph and 50px below the one above it, where before, it was... wait, it WASN'T 0px away from the paragraphs before! That's because each <p> has its own margin. This is that vertical overlapping I was talking about earlier, and paragraphs are actually a great lens through which to understand it. Suppose that, in a world without vertically overlapping margins, you want a 10px margin above and below your paragraphs. You could just set those margins to 10px. Then there would be 10px space above the first paragraph and 10px below the last, but there would be 20px in the middle! You could give the first and last paragraphs individual stylings with a class, but that's a huge, annoying hassle, especially if that ordering ever changes. Thus, vertically overlapping margins.
