Abstract, In Any Case

Alex Birdsall
6.2.2014

According to the test, my dominant thinking style is very nearly a tie between Abstract Sequential and Abstract Random. The results, tallied up, claim that Abstract Random has a slight edge over Abstract Sequential; subjectively, I'd give the edge to Abstract Sequential, but however you slice it, it's functionally a tie.

Looking through the list of learning preferences for the abstract thinking styles, it's striking how big a divergence there is with regard to group work. I mean, just compare the two:

Abstract Sequential:
  • his/her point to be heard
  • analyzing situations before making a decision or acting
  • applying logic in solving or finding solutions to problems
Abstract Random:
  • to listen to others
  • bringing harmony to group situations
  • establishing healthy relationships with others
  • focusing on the issues at hand

Random's a total team player, while Sequential's kind of ambivalent. Here, I identify a bit more with the sequential characteristics. I like a logical plan of attack, and I like sometimes to just put my head down and figure things out for myself. And I definitely dislike having my suggestions ignored. But I also enjoy working in a group, and when I am, respect and listening are just inherent to the process. Blowing off my partner's work or suggestions feels totally unnatural to me: if I dislike the contribution, I try to clearly and respectfully explain why I think we should do something else, but acknowledging it is just fundamental.

Going through the lists of "What's hard for them"s, I really identify with a grab bag of traits from various styles:

  • Abstract Sequential:
    • Too little time to deal with a subject thoroughly
    • Repeating the same tasks over and over
    • "sentimental" thinking
    • Not monopolizing a conversation
  • Abstract Random:
    • Working with dictatorial/authoritarian personalities
    • Working with people who don’t seem friendly
  • Concrete Random:
    • Re-doing anything once it’s done
    • Keeping detailed records
  • Concrete Sequential:
    • Working in an unorganized environment
The problems that have cropped up so far, or which seem likely to crop up in while working through Dev Bootcamp, are insufficient time, not monopolizing tasks when I feel I'm the one with the answer, and re-doing anything once it's done (refactoring, I see you there). I try to be diplomatic and self-aware even when monopolizing a conversation, so that's just a matter of building good collaboration-specific habits; the others feel thornier. I like to keep on a subject until I understand it as well as I'd like; I've noticed a tendency to spend a disproportionate amount of time on each week's earliest challenges until I'm in a time-crunch and have to hustle through the end of the week. And my aversion to re-doing work has lead to a lifetime of phoning in revisions. I'm both structurally- and detail-oriented, so I tend to work with a sort of "running edit" that results in a time-consuming but fairly polished first draft; that makes it hard to go back and find the things that need changing, because all the obvious ones have been changed already. Still, I think I will find it a challenge to maintain some kind of habit of revision, so that I'm not starting from square one when I start working on more complex projects that can't just be polished up on the fly.

In view of all this, my personal evolution here is going to have to focus on getting more comfortable with Concrete Sequential behaviors: process, routine, and consistency. They don't come comfortably for me, so I'm going to have to strengthen those mental muscles intentionally. Things like demanding a sincere engagement with the refactoring process even when it seems trivial in the context of a given project are going to pay off for me down the line.
